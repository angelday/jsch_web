Design case study: Mapillary

Mapillary is an open mapping platform that turns street-level imagery into map data, using computer vision and tools for human review. Uploaded street-view images are positioned spatially, while features like traffic signs are automatically extracted and placed as coordinates on the map, making map editing easier.

I experienced the company’s growth from its early years through its acquisition in 2020.

As an early designer at the startup, my responsibilities spanned product design for Android, iOS, and web, as well as brand work, conference materials, hiring, mentorship, and design system governance.

Scaling data collection

Even with automation in place, maps were still edited and verified by humans at scale. One way to collect high-quality, up-to-date street-level imagery for map editing is to commission drivers and direct them to capture specific areas.

Years: 2019–2020
Scope: driver app, admin tooling, and the end-to-end capture workflow
Role: Design principal — leading the design direction and contributing hands-on across the product
Outcome: shipped product, successful US pilot, and revenue-generating capture projects

My contribution

  • Shaped the product direction and release scope
  • Designed the admin workflow for projects, tasks, and drivers
  • Designed the driver app experience from first concept to implementation
  • Tested the workflow by dogfooding live capture projects
  • Turned operational findings into practical product improvements
  • Supported implementation with specs, review, and iteration

I narrowed the problem down to the essentials, so the first release could deliver value while leaving room for future iterations.

Admin: add/remove/setup projects, add/remove drivers
Project: assign/review tasks, driver management
Drivers app: capture, pick up/reject tasks

Admin UI

To show driver contributions on the map, I chose not to color-code individual tracks. Instead, we introduced driver avatars and sidebar filtering, making it possible to view each person’s contribution.

Drivers app

A key part of the system was a mobile app that helped drivers carry out capture tasks. This new app was designed and built from the ground up. I chose a horizontal orientation because drivers usually captured imagery that way.

I mapped out how a simple action, such as removing a driver from a project, could affect the experience for both admins and drivers.

I created focused, simplified product screens throughout the design process to explain what we could expect from the user experience.

Testing

Dogfooding is a great way to learn. I spent several days doing a manager’s work on a live project: contacting drivers, checking on progress, accepting, and rejecting tasks.

This hands-on experience helped me identify and address several low-hanging-fruit issues, including task display, links to profiles, distance from the task center point, driver home location, and approve/reject colors.

I discovered usability problems around task acceptance, and the recording indicator was not performing well either.

Exploring further

We released a first version and ran several successful capture projects for organizations. During that time, we explored ways to make data collection more effortless.

One direction was sawtoothing: a driving pattern for covering the streets of an area more efficiently.

To create an illustration of an expected driving route for an onboarding video, I observed an actual image sequence from one of our drivers and recreated the exact movement, technically by importing the GPS trace into After Effects. This helped us think about how task routing could be automated.
